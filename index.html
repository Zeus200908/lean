        <section id="philosophy">

            <div class="text-center">

                <h3>The 'Why': A New Philosophy</h3>

                <p style="max-width: 42rem; margin: auto;">

                    Lean isn't just a set of tools; it's a mindset built on two powerful pillars. This section introduces the foundational principles that drive a Lean transformation.

                </p>

            </div>

            <div class="grid md-grid-cols-2" style="max-width: 64rem; margin: 2rem auto;">

                <div class="card">

                    <h4 class="text-cyan-700">Jidoka: Built-in Quality</h4>

                    <p>Often called &quot;automation with a human touch,&quot; Jidoka is about embedding quality at every step. It empowers any employee to stop production the moment a defect is found, preventing waste and forcing immediate problem-solving.</p>

                    <div class="info-box">

                        <p class="font-semibold">In Your Print Shop:</p>

                        <p>An operator sees banding on the first banner of a 100-piece run and stops the printer immediately, saving 99 banners from being wasted.</p>

                    </div>

                </div>

                <div class="card">

                    <h4 class="text-cyan-700">Just-in-Time (JIT): Flow</h4>

                    <p>JIT's goal is to create a smooth, continuous flow of work. The principle is simple: make only what is needed, when it is needed, and in the amount needed. This eliminates massive overproduction and inventory waste.</p>

                    <div class="info-box">

                        <p class="font-semibold">In Your Print Shop:</p>

                        <p>A customer order &quot;pulls&quot; a finished sign from shipping, which in turn pulls the sign from finishing, which pulls the print from the press. No work is done until it's truly needed.</p>

                    </div>

                </div>

            </div>

        </section>


        <section id="waste">

            <div class="text-center">

                <h3>The 'What': Learning to See Waste</h3>

                <p style="max-width: 42rem; margin: auto;">

                    Waste (*Muda*) is any activity that consumes resources but adds no value for the customer. The first step to eliminating waste is learning to see it. Explore the eight wastes below.

                </p>

            </div>

            <div style="max-width: 64rem; margin: 2rem auto;">

                <div class="waste-card-static card">

                    <h4 class="text-cyan-700">🚚 Transportation</h4>

                    <p><strong>Definition:</strong> Unnecessary movement of products, materials, or information between processes.</p>

                    <div class="info-box"><p><strong>Example:</strong> Moving heavy vinyl rolls from a disorganized storage area to a printer at the other end of the building.</p></div>

                </div>

                <div class="waste-card-static card">

                    <h4 class="text-cyan-700">📦 Inventory</h4>

                    <p><strong>Definition:</strong> Any raw materials, work-in-progress (WIP), or finished goods in excess of what is required for the immediate next step.</p>

                    <div class="info-box"><p><strong>Example:</strong> Overstocking on specialty inks and substrates &quot;just in case,&quot; tying up cash and space.</p></div>

                </div>

                <div class="waste-card-static card">

                    <h4 class="text-cyan-700">🏃 Motion</h4>

                    <p><strong>Definition:</strong> Unnecessary movement of people or equipment within a process.</p>

                    <div class="info-box"><p><strong>Example:</strong> An operator repeatedly walking back and forth between the printer's control panel and the RIP computer.</p></div>

                </div>

                <div class="waste-card-static card">

                    <h4 class="text-cyan-700">⏳ Waiting</h4>

                    <p><strong>Definition:</strong> Idle time created when people, machines, or materials are not ready for the next step.</p>

                    <div class="info-box"><p><strong>Example:</strong> A $100,000 printer sitting idle while waiting for a customer to approve a digital proof.</p></div>

                </div>

                <div class="waste-card-static card">

                    <h4 class="text-cyan-700">🏭 Overproduction</h4>

                    <p><strong>Definition:</strong> Producing more of something than is needed by the next process, or producing it sooner than it is needed.</p>

                    <div class="info-box"><p><strong>Example:</strong> Printing a large banner job days before it is due, which then clutters the finishing area.</p></div>

                </div>

                <div class="waste-card-static card">

                    <h4 class="text-cyan-700">⚙️ Over-processing</h4>

                    <p><strong>Definition:</strong> Performing more work or adding more features to a product than the customer requires or values.</p>

                    <div class="info-box"><p><strong>Example:</strong> Using the highest possible print resolution for a billboard that will only be viewed from hundreds of feet away.</p></div>

                </div>

                <div class="waste-card-static card">

                    <h4 class="text-cyan-700">💔 Defects</h4>

                    <p><strong>Definition:</strong> Errors, mistakes, scrap, or rework required to make a product or service meet customer expectations.</p>

                    <div class="info-box"><p><strong>Example:</strong> Color mismatches requiring a full reprint. Banding, skewed prints, or ink smudges.</p></div>

                </div>

                <div class="waste-card-static card">

                    <h4 class="text-cyan-700">🧠 Skills</h4>

                    <p><strong>Definition:</strong> Non-utilized talent. Failing to engage employees, listen to their ideas, and use their full knowledge and creativity.</p>

                    <div class="info-box"><p><strong>Example:</strong> A top-down culture where only managers make process decisions.</p></div>

                </div>

            </div>

        </section>

            <div class="text-center">

                <h3>The 'How': Building an Improvement Culture</h3>

                <p style="max-width: 42rem; margin: auto;">

                    Paul Akers' &quot;2 Second Lean&quot; simplifies Lean into a powerful, people-first system. The goal is to &quot;grow people&quot; who then improve processes through simple, daily habits.

                </p>

            </div>

            <div class="grid md-grid-cols-3" style="max-width: 80rem; margin: 2rem auto;">

                <div class="card text-center"><div class="icon">🧐</div><h4>1. See Waste Daily</h4><p>Start with the simple mantra: &quot;Fix what bugs you.&quot; Daily frustrations are symptoms of waste, making it personal and immediate.</p></div>

                <div class="card text-center"><div class="icon">⚡️</div><h4>2. Make 2-Second Improvements</h4><p>Lower the bar for improvement to a tiny, achievable level. A small fix is not intimidating and builds a daily habit of action.</p></div>

                <div class="card text-center"><div class="icon">📲</div><h4>3. Document & Share</h4><p>Use a smartphone to make simple &quot;before and after&quot; videos. This celebrates contributions and shares knowledge effortlessly.</p></div>

            </div>

            <div class="card" style="max-width: 48rem; margin: 2rem auto;">

                <h4 class="text-center">The Power of Compounding Improvements</h4>

                <h3>The 'Now': Your Implementation Blueprint</h3>

                <p style="max-width: 48rem; margin: auto;">

                    This is your roadmap. It bridges philosophy with action, using &quot;2 Second Lean&quot; as the on-ramp to a full Lean transformation.

                </p>

            </div>

                    <div class="timeline-content">

                        <p>First 30 Days</p>

                        <h4>Phase 1: Build Cultural Habits</h4>

                        <p>This initial phase is entirely focused on leadership commitment and establishing core routines.</p>

                        <ul>

                            <li>Your Personal Commitment: Read &quot;2 Second Lean&quot; and start improving your own work.</li>

                            <li>Go and See (Genchi Genbutsu): Walk the shop floor to understand the current reality.</li>

                            <li>Launch the Daily Morning Meeting: Start a simple, 15-minute daily team huddle.</li>

                            <li>Introduce 3S & &quot;Fix What Bugs You&quot;: Dedicate time for Sort, Sweep, Standardize.</li>

                            <li>Make the First Videos: As the leader, film your own improvement to show how it's done.</li>

                        </ul>

                    </div>

                </div>

                <div class="timeline-item">

                    <div class="timeline-dot"></div>

                    <div class="timeline-content">

                        <p>Months 2-6</p>

                        <h4>Phase 2: Visualize the Workflow & Hunt Waste</h4>

                        <p>With basic habits established, the team is ready to look at the business more systematically.</p>

                        <ul>

                            <li>Map Your Core Workflow: On a whiteboard, map the high-level steps from order to delivery.</li>

                            <li>Apply the Eight Wastes: Use the map to discuss where each of the 8 wastes occurs.</li>

                            <li>Create a Waste Matrix: Build a visual &quot;heat map&quot; of where waste is most prevalent.</li>

                            <li>Empower Teams: Encourage teams to make daily improvements in their specific areas.</li>

                        </ul>

                    </div>

                </div>

                <div class="timeline-item" style="padding-bottom: 0;">

                    <div class="timeline-dot"></div>

                    <div class="timeline-content">

                        <p>Months 6-12+</p>

                        <h4>Phase 3: Lock in Gains & Tackle Complexity</h4>

                        <p>Once the culture of daily improvement is strong, introduce more formal Lean systems.</p>

                        <ul>

                            <li>Establish Standardized Work: Document the new best practices with simple, visual guides.</li>

                            <li>Implement Visual Controls: Use Kanban systems for consumables and a physical job board.</li>

                            <li>Move Towards a &quot;Pull&quot; System: Limit Work-in-Progress (WIP) to smooth the flow.</li>

                            <li>Give Tours: Build pride and gain new insights by showing your progress to outsiders.</li>

                        </ul>
